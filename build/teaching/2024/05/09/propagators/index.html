Wavefront Propagation

May 9, 2024 · 4 min read
Yuri R. Tonin
Physics Engineer

This post intends to be a quick guide on the most important equations for computing free space propagation of a wavefront in the Fresnel regime, and how to do it properly.

Helmholtz equation and elementary waves​

First, some background. Consider a monochromatic wave ψ\psiψ in vacuum. It is governed by Helmholtz equation:

∇2ψ+k2ψ=0\nabla^2\psi + k^2 \psi = 0 ∇2ψ+k2ψ=0

which has two solutions which we call elementary waves. These are:

Plane Waves

ψ(r)=Aexp⁡(−jk⋅r)=Aexp⁡[−j(kxx+kyy+kzz)]\psi(\mathbf{r}) = A \exp{(-j \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{r})} = A \exp{[-j (k_x x + k_y y + k_z z)]}ψ(r)=Aexp(−jk⋅r)=Aexp[−j(kx​x+ky​y+kz​z)]

Spherical Waves

ψ(r)=Arexp⁡(−jkr)\psi(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{A}{r} \exp{(-j k r)} ψ(r)=rA​exp(−jkr)

which names indicates that the wavefronts (i.e. the surfaces of constant phase) are either planes or spheres. These are the most basic waves there are. Keep in mind that spherical waves are the kind of wave produced by point sources.

Naturally, what we encounter waves that are way more complex than elementary waves. Luckily, we can make things easier by what is called the Angular Spectrum of waves.

When to use which?​

Now, to the practical side: how do you choose which to use? In that case one may use a rule of thumb (although for the details I'd recommend taking a close look at the references below):

CriteriaSamplingComment
Δx>λzL\Delta x > \frac{\lambda z}{L}Δx>Lλz​TF: oversampled. IR: undersampledIR method causes periodic copies. TF is the method of choice. Observation plane width will be limited to width D+λz/ΔxD + \lambda z / \Delta xD+λz/Δx
Δx=λzL\Delta x = \frac{\lambda z}{L}Δx=Lλz​both TF and IR: critical samplingTF and IR are identical
Δx<λzL\Delta x < \frac{\lambda z}{L}Δx<Lλz​TF: undersampled. IR: oversampledIt depends. Needs to evaluate if source bandwidth satisfies B≤L2λzB \le \frac{L}{2 \lambda z}B≤2λzL​. If yes, use TF. Otherwise, IR may be better.
